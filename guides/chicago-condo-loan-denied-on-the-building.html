Chicago Condo Mortgages: Why Your Loan Got Denied on the Building, Not You

Short answer: When you finance a condo, the lender underwrites two separate things — you and the condo association. A denial with strong credit, income, and assets almost always means the building failed the project review, not you. In Chicago the usual culprits are commercial space in mixed-use buildings, HOA litigation, thin reserves, high dues delinquency, deferred maintenance, or one owner holding too many units. The right next step is to read the condo questionnaire to find the exact trigger — then decide whether it's a fixable documentation gap or a case for a portfolio or non-QM condo loan.

Why a condo loan gets denied on the building

A single-family home loan underwrites one thing: you. A condo loan underwrites two — you (credit, income, assets) and the condo project (the association's finances, legal status, and physical condition). You can be a flawless borrower and still get a denial because the building doesn't meet the lender's project guidelines.

This is called the condo project review, and it's driven largely by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac ("GSE") rules for any conventional loan the lender wants to sell to them. If the association passes, the building is warrantable and you get standard conventional financing. If it fails, the building is non-warrantable, and a conventional loan can't be used — no matter how strong you are on paper.

So when a buyer says "my loan was denied but my credit is 780 and I put 25% down," the denial usually has nothing to do with the borrower. It's the building.

The building-level reasons a Chicago condo loan gets denied

Chicago's housing stock — vintage courtyard buildings, mixed-use corridors, deconverted apartments — makes building-based denials more common here than in many markets. The recurring triggers:

Commercial space in mixed-use buildings

Chicago is full of buildings with a storefront, restaurant, or office on the ground floor and condos above. When non-residential use takes up too much of the building's floor area, the project fails warrantability. Retail-dense corridors — Clark Street in Lakeview, Milwaukee Avenue in Wicker Park, Southport, Andersonville, Lincoln Avenue — are where this comes up most.

HOA litigation

If the association is party to certain lawsuits — especially over construction defects or structural/safety issues — the project is typically non-warrantable until it's resolved. Routine matters (a minor slip-and-fall covered by insurance) usually don't disqualify a building, but structural cases do.

Thin reserves and deferred maintenance

Lenders expect the HOA budget to fund a meaningful reserve. Vintage Chicago buildings with aging facades, roofs, porches, and tuckpointing needs sometimes fall short. Since 2021, the GSEs have tightened scrutiny on significant deferred maintenance, unsafe conditions, and large special assessments — a building with major unaddressed structural work or a pending assessment tied to it can be flagged.

High dues delinquency

If too many owners are behind on their assessments, the association's finances are considered unstable and the project fails the review. In a small Chicago building, just one or two delinquent units can push the percentage over the limit.

Single-entity / investor concentration

If one person or entity owns an outsized share of the units, the project is non-warrantable. This shows up in deconverted or partially deconverted buildings and smaller associations where one landlord holds several units — a recurring pattern after Chicago's wave of condo deconversions.

Insurance gaps

The association's master policy has to meet coverage requirements — hazard, liability, and (where applicable) flood. An underinsured building, or one missing required coverage, can trigger a denial that surfaces late in the process.

Owner-occupancy (mainly for investment purchases)

If you're buying a unit as an investment property, GSE rules generally require a large share of the building to be owner-occupied, so a mostly-rental building can knock out the loan. Buying as a primary residence generally does not carry an owner-occupancy minimum on the building — a distinction that surprises many buyers.

Note: The specific GSE thresholds — commercial-space percentage, reserve percentage, delinquency limits, single-entity ownership cap — change over time. Kyle confirms the current figures against your specific building before you rely on them.

How to read your condo denial

A condo denial isn't a dead end — it's a clue. The first job is to find the exact reason, because the fix depends entirely on which trigger fired. The source of truth is the condo questionnaire plus the association's financials. Look for:

  1. The condo questionnaire (completed by the HOA or management company) — the specific line that failed
  2. The HOA budget and reserve study — how much is set aside
  3. Delinquency and ownership-concentration figures
  4. The master insurance certificate
  5. Any litigation, special-assessment, or major-repair disclosures

Once you know the trigger, it falls into one of two buckets.

Fixable documentation gaps

Some "denials" are really incomplete paperwork: a blank questionnaire field, a missing reserve study, an out-of-date insurance certificate, or a management company that answered a question conservatively without context. These can sometimes be cleared by getting the association or management company to complete or clarify the record.

Structural building problems

Other triggers are baked into the building: active structural litigation, a large safety-related special assessment, too much commercial space, or heavy investor concentration. You can't paperwork your way past these — but you can still finance the unit with the right program.

What to do when the building genuinely fails

When a building truly doesn't meet GSE rules, the practical path is a portfolio loan or non-QM loan — financing from a lender that keeps the loan on its own books or sells to investors with more flexible project rules. Because conventional lenders all share the same core GSE project guidelines, shopping the same loan to five conventional lenders usually produces five denials. A portfolio or non-QM lender reviews the building on its own criteria and can often close a unit a conventional lender declined.

What to generally expect versus a standard conforming loan:

  • A larger down payment. Non-warrantable programs typically ask for more equity — often in the range of 10–25%+ depending on the program, building, and occupancy type.
  • A building-specific review. The lender evaluates the association on its own guidelines rather than the strict GSE checklist.
  • Pricing that varies by program. Terms depend on the lender, the building, and your profile. (No rate is quoted here — ask for a current, personalized Loan Estimate.)

How to avoid the denial in the first place

The best time to catch a building problem is before you're under contract — not two weeks before closing. Have your loan officer pull and review the condo questionnaire, HOA budget, reserve study, insurance, and any litigation or special-assessment disclosures early. That review tells you whether the building is warrantable and, if it isn't, lets you line up the right program so the offer you make is one you can actually close.

FAQ

Why did my Chicago condo loan get denied when my credit and income are strong?

Condo loans underwrite both you and the building. Even with perfect finances, the loan can be declined if the association fails the project review — commercial space, litigation, low reserves, high dues delinquency, or single-entity ownership. The denial is about the building, not you.

What is a condo project review or condo questionnaire?

It's the lender's evaluation of the association itself: budget, reserves, insurance, owner-occupancy, ownership concentration, litigation, and special assessments. The condo questionnaire is the form the HOA or management company completes so the lender can confirm the project meets guidelines.

Can I do anything to fix a condo denial caused by the building?

Sometimes. Some issues are documentation gaps the HOA can correct; others — active structural litigation, a large special assessment — are structural to the building. When the building genuinely fails GSE rules, the practical path is a portfolio or non-QM condo loan rather than trying to change the association.

Does a denial from one lender mean every lender will deny it?

Not necessarily. Conventional (GSE) lenders share the same core project rules, so if a building fails those, most conventional loans will fail too. But portfolio and non-QM lenders review buildings on their own guidelines and can often finance a project a conventional lender declined.

How can I check a Chicago building before I make an offer?

Have your loan officer review the condo questionnaire, HOA budget and reserve study, owner-occupancy and ownership-concentration figures, insurance, and any litigation or special-assessment disclosures before you go under contract — so a building problem doesn't surface days before closing.

Is a special assessment enough to get a condo loan denied?

It can be, especially one tied to structural or safety repairs or evidence of significant deferred maintenance. A modest, well-funded assessment is often manageable, but the lender will look at what it's for and whether the building's finances are stable.
